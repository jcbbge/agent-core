# Detailed Frameworks for Product Evaluation

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework

### Core Concept

Customers don't buy products - they "hire" them to make progress in specific circumstances. Understanding the job reveals true user value and competitive landscape.

### The Three Dimensions

Every job has three interconnected dimensions:

**1. Functional Dimension**
- The practical task that needs accomplishing
- Observable, measurable outcome
- Example: "Transfer files between my devices"

**2. Social Dimension**
- How this affects others' perception of the user
- Status, identity, belonging concerns
- Example: "Look tech-savvy to my colleagues"

**3. Emotional Dimension**
- How the user feels about the situation
- Anxiety, confidence, security concerns
- Example: "Feel in control of my digital life"

**Critical insight**: Products that address all three dimensions create stronger value propositions than those focused only on functional jobs.

### Forces of Progress

Four forces influence whether someone will switch to your product:

**Push forces** (toward change):
- Current solution pain points
- Frustration with status quo
- External pressure or circumstances

**Pull forces** (toward new solution):
- Attraction to new product benefits
- Aspirational outcomes
- Compelling value proposition

**Anxiety forces** (holding back):
- Fear of making wrong choice
- Uncertainty about new solution
- Cost of learning new system

**Habit forces** (inertia):
- Familiarity with current approach
- Switching costs (time, money, effort)
- Established workflows and patterns

**For adoption**: Pull + Push must exceed Anxiety + Habit

### Finding the Job

**Interview questions to uncover the job:**

1. "Walk me through the last time you [struggled with this problem]"
2. "What were you trying to accomplish?"
3. "What did you try before settling on [current solution]?"
4. "What would have to happen for you to stop using [current solution]?"
5. "How does solving this make you feel?"

**Look for:**
- The "struggling moment" - specific circumstance triggering the need
- Desired outcome - what does "progress" look like?
- Alternative solutions tried - reveals true competition
- Emotional language - indicates social and emotional dimensions

### Classic JTBD Examples

**Morning Milkshake (McDonald's)**
- Circumstance: Boring, long morning commute
- Functional: Keep me full until lunch
- Social: Easier than eating messy food while driving
- Emotional: Small pleasure to start the day
- Competition: Bananas (too quick), bagels (messy), donuts (guilt)
- Solution: Thick milkshake (lasts whole commute, easy to consume, guilt-free treat)

**Condominium Sales**
- Circumstance: Downsizing after kids leave home
- Functional: Smaller home, less maintenance
- Social: Still host family gatherings (dining room table concern)
- Emotional: Don't want to feel like "giving up" life stage
- Competition: Staying in current home despite impracticality
- Solution: Full moving service + storage = address emotional barrier

**Key lesson**: Demographics don't predict behavior - circumstances do. Morning commuters and afternoon treat-seekers are different jobs, even for the same product.

### Applying JTBD to Your Idea

1. **Define the circumstance**: When does this problem occur?
2. **Map all three dimensions**: Functional + Social + Emotional
3. **Identify ALL alternatives**: What do users currently do?
4. **Find the struggling moment**: What triggers the need?
5. **Describe desired progress**: What does success look like?

**Red flag**: If you can only describe functional dimension, dig deeper. The social and emotional dimensions often differentiate your product from alternatives.

## Product-Market Fit (PMF) Measurement

### Core Principle

PMF is not a binary state or milestone - it's a continuous trajectory. Measure it as dynamic evolution using both quantitative behavior and qualitative sentiment.

### Quantitative PMF Indicators

**1. Retention Curves (Most Important)**

Retention is the single best PMF indicator because it reveals whether your product delivers ongoing value.

**Key metrics:**
- **Day-7 retention**: 40%+ indicates strong initial value
- **Day-30 retention**: 20%+ shows sustained engagement
- **Flattening curve**: When retention stops declining sharply, you've found value

**Analysis method:**
- Track cohorts (users who joined in same week/month)
- Plot retention over time for each cohort
- Compare cohorts: Are newer cohorts better? (PMF improving)
- Look for "smile curve": Initial drop, then flattening = sticky value

**Interpretation:**
- Steep decline to near-zero = no PMF, users don't find value
- Gradual decline then plateau = PMF exists, optimize from here
- Improving cohorts = PMF trajectory positive

**2. Sean Ellis PMF Survey**

Survey active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"

Options:
- Very disappointed (40%+ = PMF achieved)
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed

**Why 40% threshold?**
- Empirically validated across hundreds of startups
- Correlates with sustainable growth
- Below 40% = still searching for PMF

**Implementation:**
- Survey users who have used product at least twice
- Minimum 30-40 responses for statistical relevance
- Track over time: Is percentage increasing?

**3. Net Promoter Score (NPS)**

"How likely are you to recommend [product] to a friend?" (0-10 scale)

- Promoters (9-10): Enthusiastic advocates
- Passives (7-8): Satisfied but unenthusiastic
- Detractors (0-6): Unhappy, may churn

**NPS = % Promoters - % Detractors**

**PMF correlation:**
- NPS 50+: Strong PMF signal
- NPS 0-50: Some value, needs improvement
- NPS negative: Major PMF issues

**4. Feature Engagement Depth**

Track how deeply users engage with core features:

- What % of users complete the core job?
- How many core actions per session?
- Are users exploring adjacent features?

**Example (meal planning app):**
- Core job: Generate meal plan
- Depth indicators: Save recipes, create shopping list, repeat plans
- PMF signal: 70%+ users complete full planning workflow

### Qualitative PMF Signals

**Strong PMF indicators:**
- Users describe product as "indispensable" or "can't live without"
- Spontaneous referrals without prompting
- Users find creative use cases you didn't anticipate
- Support requests for power user features
- Community forming around product

**Weak PMF indicators:**
- Users struggle to articulate what problem you solve
- Referrals only happen when incentivized
- Feature requests focus on competitive parity
- High churn despite trying new features
- Users need frequent reminders to re-engage

### Hybrid Framework: Quantitative + Qualitative

**Best practice**: Combine both approaches for accurate PMF assessment

1. **Quantitative baseline**: Track retention and survey metrics
2. **Qualitative validation**: Interview high and low engagement users
3. **Cohort comparison**: Are newer cohorts improving on both?
4. **Segment analysis**: Does PMF exist for specific user segments?

**Example evaluation:**

```
Quantitative:
- Day-7 retention: 35% (below 40% target)
- Sean Ellis: 38% "very disappointed" (below 40% threshold)
- NPS: 42 (moderate)

Qualitative:
- High-retention users: "Essential daily tool"
- Low-retention users: "Interesting but not necessary"

Conclusion: PMF exists for power users, need to expand value to broader audience
Action: Focus on activation, improve onboarding, double down on power user segment
```

### PMF Evolution Stages

**Stage 1: No PMF** (Retention drops to near-zero)
- Focus: Customer discovery, find real pain point
- Action: Interview users who churned, understand why

**Stage 2: Weak PMF** (Some retention, <40% Ellis)
- Focus: Deepen value for retained users
- Action: Understand what retained segment values differently

**Stage 3: Moderate PMF** (Flattening retention, 30-50% Ellis)
- Focus: Expand to adjacent use cases, improve activation
- Action: Optimize onboarding, reduce time-to-value

**Stage 4: Strong PMF** (Stable high retention, >50% Ellis)
- Focus: Scale, growth loops, efficiency
- Action: Build sustainable acquisition channels

**Critical**: Don't scale (stage 4) before achieving at least moderate PMF (stage 3). Scaling without PMF amplifies problems.

## Growth Loops Framework

### Core Concept

Traditional funnels are linear (spend money → acquire users → some convert). Growth loops are circular systems where outputs reinvest to create more inputs, generating compounding growth.

### Why Loops Beat Funnels

**Funnels:**
- Linear acquisition (spend more → get more)
- Hit ceiling when CAC > LTV
- Fragile to market changes
- Growth dependent on continuous external investment

**Loops:**
- Compounding acquisition (users bring users)
- Sustainable without external funding
- Resilient to market shifts
- Growth embedded in product usage

### Types of Growth Loops

**1. User-Generated Content (UGC) Loops**

Mechanism: Users create content → Content attracts new users → New users create more content

**Examples:**
- Pinterest: Pins attract Google searchers → Searchers create boards → More pins
- YouTube: Videos attract viewers → Viewers become creators → More videos
- Yelp: Reviews attract diners → Diners write reviews → More coverage

**Key success factors:**
- Content must be discoverable (SEO, social sharing)
- Creation must be easy (low friction)
- Network effects (more content = more value)

**2. Viral/Referral Loops**

Mechanism: Users invite others → New users experience value → New users invite more

**Examples:**
- Dropbox: Extra storage for referrals → Users invite friends → More users
- Figma: Collaboration requires invites → Invitees become users → Team expansion
- Calendly: Sharing links invites recipients → Recipients become users

**Key success factors:**
- Product requires collaboration/sharing to deliver value
- Invites are natural byproduct of usage
- Viral coefficient >1 (each user brings >1 new user)

**3. Product-Led Growth (PLG) Loops**

Mechanism: Free users get value → Upgrade or expand → Bring teams → More free users

**Examples:**
- Notion: Free personal use → Upgrade for team features → Team members join
- Slack: Free for small teams → Upgrade for history/integrations → Org-wide adoption
- Zoom: Free meetings → Paid for longer calls → Meeting invites bring users

**Key success factors:**
- Free tier delivers real value
- Natural expansion trigger (team, usage limits)
- Viral within organizations

**4. Content/SEO Loops**

Mechanism: Create content → Ranks in search → Attracts users → Users create content signal

**Examples:**
- HubSpot: Blog posts rank → Attract marketers → Marketers share → More authority
- Moz: SEO tools → Create reports → Reports rank → Attract SEO professionals
- Zapier: Integration guides → Rank for "[app] integration" → Users discover platform

**Key success factors:**
- Content addresses high-intent search queries
- Product naturally generates more content
- Content compounds (older content continues driving value)

**5. Paid Loops** (Less common for indie devs)

Mechanism: Acquire users with ads → Users generate revenue → Reinvest in more ads

**Examples:**
- Mobile games: Ads acquire players → Players make purchases → Reinvest in UA
- E-commerce: Facebook ads → Sales → Scale ad spend

**Key success factors:**
- LTV significantly exceeds CAC
- Predictable conversion rates
- Fast payback period

### Choosing Your Primary Loop

**For solo indie developers, prioritize:**

1. **Product-Led Growth** (if applicable)
   - Easiest to implement alone
   - No content creation burden
   - Scales with product usage

2. **Content/SEO Loops** (if you like writing)
   - Sustainable long-term
   - Low ongoing cost
   - Compounds over time

3. **Viral Loops** (if product naturally encourages sharing)
   - Highest potential coefficient
   - Requires built-in sharing mechanics
   - Can be winner-take-all

**Avoid as solo dev:**
- Paid loops (requires capital)
- Complex UGC loops (requires moderation, community management)

### Loop Health Metrics

**For any loop, track:**

1. **Loop cycle time**: How long from user action to new user acquisition?
2. **Amplification factor**: How many new users does each user generate?
3. **Loop retention**: Do users continue participating in loop mechanism?
4. **Compounding rate**: Is each cycle bigger than the last?

**Example (Product-Led Loop):**
```
Metric: Free user → Team invite → Paid team
Cycle time: 14 days (average time to invite teammates)
Amplification: 2.3 users per activation (each active user brings 2.3 teammates)
Retention: 65% of teams still active after 90 days
Compounding: Each cohort 15% larger than previous

Result: Strong compounding loop with 2.3x amplification every 14 days
```

### Identifying Your Loop

**Questions to ask:**

1. Does product usage naturally create an artifact others see? → UGC/Content loop potential
2. Does product require collaboration to deliver value? → Viral/PLG loop potential
3. Can product be used for free with clear upgrade path? → PLG loop potential
4. Does product solve searchable problems? → Content/SEO loop potential

**Red flag**: If you can't identify ANY loop mechanism, growth will be purely linear through external acquisition (expensive and unsustainable for solo devs).

### Building Your Loop

**Step 1: Identify the natural loop** (don't force it)
**Step 2: Measure current loop health** (even if weak)
**Step 3: Optimize one part** (cycle time, amplification, or retention)
**Step 4: Monitor compounding** (is it growing over time?)
**Step 5: Double down** (when working, invest more)

**Example evolution:**

```
Product: Code snippet sharing tool

Initial state:
- Users save snippets privately
- No loop (pure linear acquisition)

Add loop mechanism:
- Make snippets publicly shareable with SEO-friendly URLs
- Snippets rank for "[language] [problem] solution"

Measure loop:
- Cycle time: 30 days (time for snippet to rank)
- Amplification: 0.3 (each snippet brings 0.3 new users)
- Result: Weak but positive loop

Optimize amplification:
- Improve snippet SEO (better titles, descriptions)
- Add social sharing buttons
- Create snippet collections for common problems

Improved metrics:
- Cycle time: 30 days (unchanged)
- Amplification: 1.2 (each snippet brings 1.2 new users)
- Result: Compounding loop (>1.0 amplification)
```

### Common Loop Mistakes

1. **Too many minor loops, no major loop**
   - Better: One strong loop than five weak ones
   
2. **Forcing loops that don't fit product**
   - Loop must be natural byproduct of usage
   
3. **Ignoring loop retention**
   - Loop must be sustainable, not one-time spike
   
4. **Optimizing wrong part of loop**
   - Focus on biggest constraint first

5. **Not measuring loop health**
   - Can't improve what you don't measure

**Remember**: The best growth loop is one embedded naturally in product usage, not bolted on as growth hack.
